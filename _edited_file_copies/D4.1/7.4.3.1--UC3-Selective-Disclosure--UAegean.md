#### Selective Disclosure

### 7.4 Selective Disclosure and User Consent

In **UC3: Seamless Digital Traveller Experience (SEDIT-X)**, selective disclosure is a core design principle used to ensure that WP4 relying parties receive only the minimum set of attributes necessary for each transaction. Rather than disclosing full identity records by default, the EUDI Wallet enables the user to share only the information required for the specific operational step being performed.

This is particularly important in UC3 because the traveller interacts with multiple relying parties across different domains — including **airport**, **ferry transport**, **hospitality**, **urban mobility**, and **university access** — and each of these contexts requires a different subset of information.

For example:

- at the **airport**, the relying party may need proof of identity, proof of boarding entitlement, and in some variants a biometric-related reference for 1:1 verification;
- in **ferry transport**, the operator may need only the attributes required to confirm boarding entitlement and, where relevant, vehicle or discount eligibility;
- in **hospitality**, the hotel may need the verified identity attributes required by law or operational policy together with a booking reference;
- in **university access**, the verifier may need only proof of student or academic status rather than full personal identity details.

Accordingly, UC3 does not treat credential presentation as an “all-or-nothing” disclosure model. Instead, the EUDI Wallet is used to support **context-specific presentation** of the minimum necessary claims.

Operationally, selective disclosure in UC3 is implemented through wallet-mediated presentation flows in which:

1. the relying party requests a defined set of attributes or proofs relevant to the business step;
2. the wallet shows the request to the user in a transparent way;
3. the user reviews what is being requested and provides consent to the disclosure; and
4. the wallet presents only the approved attributes or derived proofs needed for that transaction.

This means that a traveller does not need to disclose the same full dataset repeatedly throughout the journey. Different relying parties receive different data depending on their role and legal or operational need.
