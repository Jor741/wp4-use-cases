#### Electronic Attestations of Attributes (EAA / QEAA)


In the SEDIT-X use case, the **Person Identification Data (PID)** credential remains the root identity credential for establishing the traveller’s identity, but it is **not** the only credential relevant to the pilot. Across the five SEDIT-X episodes, the wallet also carries a set of **non-PID attestations** that express specific travel entitlements, booking state, student status, accessibility-related rights, vehicle-related information, and, in some variants, payment or service outcomes. For the purposes of this section, these non-PID credentials are treated as the relevant **Electronic Attestations of Attributes (EAAs)** used in the pilot. Where a given attestation is issued by a qualified trust service provider under the applicable eIDAS framework, it could additionally qualify as a **Qualified Electronic Attestation of Attributes (QEAA)**. 



### Role of EAAs / QEAAs in the SEDIT-X architecture

SEDIT-X is designed as a multi-domain journey in which the traveller repeatedly needs to prove not only identity, but also **context-specific facts** such as:

- entitlement to board a specific flight or ferry;
- existence of a hotel reservation;
- eligibility for a student or accessibility-related benefit;
- linkage of a vehicle to a ferry booking;
- or proof that a payment or operational step has already been completed.

These are precisely the kinds of statements that are naturally represented as attribute attestations in the wallet. The SEDIT-X materials therefore describe a model in which the wallet becomes a common carrier of both **identity evidence** and **domain-specific attribute attestations**, allowing verifiers to request only the data needed at each operational touchpoint. This supports selective disclosure, minimisation of shared data, and a more seamless cross-domain traveller journey.

In practical terms, the non-PID attestations used in SEDIT-X are presented and verified through the same general wallet interaction model as the rest of the pilot: issuance into the wallet using an issuance flow, presentation to a verifier through wallet-mediated consent, and back-end validation by the relevant relying party or intermediary service. In the hotel documentation, this is explicitly framed in terms of standard **OpenID4VCI 1.0** issuance and **OpenID4VP 1.0** presentation/verification flows implemented through the EUDIW Intermediary Service. The broader UC3 material applies the same general model across airport, ferry, hospitality, and university episodes.

### Main EAA / QEAA types used in SEDIT-X

The SEDIT-X materials identify a number of non-PID credentials that function as EAAs in the pilot. The most relevant are the following.

#### 1. Boarding Pass Credential

The **Boarding Pass Credential** is the main air-travel attestation used in the airport episode. It represents the traveller’s entitlement to a specific flight and is used at airport touchpoints such as check-in, baggage drop-off, security, and boarding. The UC3 material describes it as a wallet-stored verifiable credential representing the passenger’s flight entitlement and notes that it is bound to the passenger identity according to the pilot policy.

From an EAA perspective, the boarding pass credential is not primarily an identity credential; it is an attestation of a **travel-related operational attribute set**. Typical attributes would include the flight context, journey entitlement, and other information needed to determine whether the passenger may proceed through the next step of the airport process. In the direct proximity-verification path, it is presented together with PID-derived evidence, while in the BioQRCode path it contributes to the prior issuance of a downstream airport journey token.

If, in a later production deployment, such a boarding entitlement were issued by an actor and under a trust framework meeting the formal requirements for a qualified attestation, then a QEAA interpretation could be considered. In the current SEDIT-X pilot documentation, however, it is better and more accurate to describe it neutrally as a **domain-specific EAA** used for aviation entitlement.

#### 2. Biometric QR Credential / Airport Journey Token

In one airport pilot path, SEDIT-X foresees issuance of a **Biometric QR Credential** (also described as an airport journey token). This is issued by airport-side functions after prior verification steps and is then used at airport checkpoints as a compact, checkpoint-readable credential. The airport paper explains that, in the BioQRCode path, the passenger first presents PID and boarding-pass evidence in order to obtain this credential, which is subsequently used during airport navigation.

This credential is best understood as a **derived attribute attestation**. It does not replace the root identity credential, but encapsulates a bounded airport-use context: the fact that the traveller has already passed a prior enrolment/binding step and may now be processed in a more streamlined way at airport checkpoints. Because it is domain-specific and operationally scoped, it fits naturally in the broader EAA category.

#### 3. Ferry Boarding Credential

The **Ferry Boarding Credential** is the maritime equivalent of the flight boarding pass. The UC3 material states that it is issued at booking time by the ferry operator or participating travel agency and stored in the traveller’s wallet for later validation and boarding. At the port, it is presented in a proximity-based flow together with the minimum additional evidence needed for the specific journey.

This credential functions as an attestation of the traveller’s **entitlement to board a particular ferry journey**. In the ferry episode, it also supports automation of discount handling and fast boarding decisions. As with the flight boarding pass, it should be treated in the current draft as a domain-specific EAA rather than given a formal QEAA label unless the trust and legal issuance model is explicitly defined as qualified.

#### 4. Booking Reference Credential

The **Booking Reference Credential** is the clearest example of a non-PID attribute attestation in the hospitality episode. The hotel material describes a sequence in which, after successful reservation creation, the booking environment triggers issuance of a credential containing the booking reference into the traveller’s EUDI Wallet via the EUDIW Intermediary Service. At the hotel, the traveller later presents this credential together with the required identity attributes so that the reservation can be retrieved and the check-in process can progress.

This is a textbook EAA-like construct within the SEDIT-X pilot. It attests to a very specific attribute set linked to the hotel booking context: namely that a reservation exists and can be referenced through a booking identifier. It is not a general identity credential; it is a **purpose-bound hospitality attestation** used to drive a concrete operational process.



#### 5. Student Status Credential

The **Student Status Credential** is one of the main non-PID credentials held in the wallet. It is used for student-related entitlements such as verified discounts and university access. In the broader SEDIT-X framing, this credential is especially relevant for travellers who are also ERASMUS/ERUA students and need to move across travel and academic service domains.

This credential is a strong example of an attribute attestation in the proper sense: it does not establish identity by itself, but attests that the holder has a recognised **student status** issued by a competent educational institution or related authority. In some real deployments this type of credential could potentially map very naturally to an EAA or, depending on the issuance regime, even a QEAA. For the current SEDIT-X text, the safest wording is that the pilot uses a student-status attestation as a non-PID wallet credential supporting discounts and academic access.

#### 6. Accessibility / Disability Attestation

 These attestations support selective disclosure of accessibility-related rights or conditions so that participating service providers can grant appropriate assistance, equal-treatment measures, or discounted conditions without requiring the traveller to expose unnecessary information.


#### 7. Mobile Vehicle Registration Credential (mVRC)

For ferry scenarios involving a vehicle, the traveller may need to present a **mobile Vehicle Registration Credential (mVRC)** or equivalent vehicle-related credential. This is used to show that the vehicle presented for embarkation is consistent with the booking context.

Within the structure of this section, the mVRC should also be treated as a non-PID attestation. It expresses attributes about a vehicle and its registration context, rather than about the traveller’s civil identity. When used in the ferry episode, it extends the SEDIT-X model from person-related entitlements to vehicle-linked entitlements.

#### 8. Payment Confirmation / e-Receipt Artefacts

These are used in scenarios such as overweight baggage fees, transport services, or hospitality extras and are not always described as core credentials in the same way as the boarding or booking-reference credentials, but they still function as attestations of a completed operational state.

However, they can later be presented as proof that a charge has been authorised or settled, they can be understood as **transactional attribute attestations**. Their role in SEDIT-X is secondary compared with boarding or booking credentials, but they remain relevant to the broader attribute-attestation landscape of the pilot.
