#### PID (Personal Identification Data)


In **SEDIT-X**, the **PID (Personal Identification Data)** credential is the foundational identity credential held by the traveller in the **EUDI Wallet**. Across the use case, PID functions as the primary trust anchor for identity assurance and is used to support identity verification, the binding of travel-related credentials to the same subject, and the selective disclosure of only those personal attributes that are necessary in a given operational context.

SEDIT-X assumes that the traveller has a valid PID stored in the wallet before the journey begins. In this sense, PID is a precondition for the use case rather than a credential issued within the SEDIT-X pilot itself. The broader UC description explicitly treats PID as the root identity credential of the journey and as the basis on which additional credentials and service interactions rely.

##### Role of PID in the overall SEDIT-X architecture

Within the SEDIT-X design, PID serves four core purposes:

1. **Identity proofing and high-assurance identification.** PID provides the verified identity basis for interactions that require the traveller to prove who they are.
2. **Binding of derived or domain-specific credentials.** Travel and service credentials such as boarding passes or booking-reference credentials are operationally linked to the same traveller identity that is represented by the PID.
3. **Selective disclosure of identity attributes.** The traveller presents only the attributes required for the transaction, rather than exposing the full credential content.
4. **Trust establishment across domains.** PID enables a common identity layer across airport, ferry, hospitality, and university-related interactions.

##### PID in Episode 1 – Smart Airport

PID has a central role in the airport episode. The airport material describes the EUDI Wallet as a core identity layer in airport operations and treats PID as the credential that allows the passenger to prove identity at key airport touchpoints. In the direct proximity-verification path, the traveller presents the **PID together with the boarding pass credential** from the wallet in order to support check-in, baggage-drop, security, and gate processes.

During flight check-in and boarding-pass issuance, the airline or airport-facing system may request a verifiable presentation of the PID in order to perform an **identity-to-booking match** and bind the boarding pass credential to the passenger identity. The airport paper is explicit that this is done with **selective disclosure**, meaning that not all PID attributes need to be released in every interaction.

In the biometric-enhanced airport flow, PID also supports the **1:1 identity-binding logic**. The pilot assumption in the airport material is that the PID contains a **portrait image / facial biometric reference**, which can support comparison against a live capture in the direct wallet presentation path. At the same time, the pilot text also stresses that biometric processing should remain privacy-preserving and ephemeral, with no persistent biometric database on the airport side.

In short, at the airport the PID is not merely another credential among many. It is the credential that anchors passenger identity, supports boarding-pass binding, and underpins privacy-preserving identity verification at multiple operational checkpoints.

##### PID in Episode 3 – Ferry Transport

In the ferry episode, PID is again used as the **root identity credential**. At the port, the intended baseline interaction is a **proximity-based presentation flow** in which the traveller presents:

- the **PID**,
- the **Ferry Boarding Credential**, and
- where relevant, an **mVRC** for a vehicle.

Here, PID is used to establish who the traveller is, while the ferry boarding credential proves the specific travel entitlement and the mVRC, where applicable, links the traveller to the declared vehicle. This means that PID provides the identity layer on top of which the ferry boarding decision is made.

The same episode also anticipates that other credentials, such as student-status or accessibility attestations, may influence discounts or boarding conditions. Even in those cases, PID remains the core credential for traveller identity, while the other credentials express entitlements or special conditions.

##### PID in Episode 4 – Hospitality

In the hotel episode, PID is used in two related stages.

First, during **booking-time identity support**, the traveller may use the wallet to present the personal identity attributes needed to complete the reservation. The hotel working paper explains that the booking portal initiates a wallet presentation flow through the EUDIW Intermediary Service and receives verified identity attributes back for use in the booking transaction.

Second, during **on-site self-service hotel check-in**, the traveller presents the required personal identification attributes together with the **Booking Reference Credential**. In this stage, PID-derived attributes are used to satisfy the hotel’s legal and operational identification requirements, while the booking reference is used to retrieve the specific reservation.

This means that, in the hospitality episode, PID is not used as the booking token itself. Rather, it is used to provide **trusted identity data**, while the booking-reference credential supplies the reservation context. The two are complementary: PID answers “who is the traveller?”, while the booking-reference credential answers “which reservation is being claimed?”.
