## Document Structure

The deliverable is organised into 9 chapters followed by a set of annexes, moving from the high-level framing of the project, through the methodological and contextual chapters, into the core body of use case specifications, and finally to the forward-looking roadmap that connects D4.1 to the implementation and validation phases. The chosen structure helps navigating directly to the sections of greatest interest. The paragraphs below describe, for each chapter, its purpose, the main questions it answers, and the audience it primarily serves.

**Chapter 1: Executive Summary** offers a concise high level overview of the deliverable. It summarises the WP4 scope, introduces the three scenarios and the 11 use cases, highlights the key findings of the stock-taking phase, and sets out the cross-border interoperability considerations that shape the pilot design.

**Chapter 2: Introduction** The present chapter positions D4.1 within the APTITUDE project. It states the purpose and scope of the deliverable (Section 2.1), recalls the WP4 objectives as declared in the Grant Agreement (Section 2.2), describes the dependencies between WP4 and the other Work Packages (Section 2.3), and, through Section 2.5, introduces the key terms used in the rest of the document. 

**Chapter 3: Methodology** describes how the content of D4.1 was produced during the stock-taking phase. It presents the overall analysis approach (Section 3.1), documents the categories of stakeholders consulted and the engagement methods used (Section 3.2), and lists the key reference documents and collaborative tools that supported the work (Section 3.3). Chapter 3.4 introduces the UC template used to detail the specifications.

**Chapter 4: Scenarios and Use Cases** is the core of the deliverable. It is structured in two layers. The first layer (Section 4.1) is a scenario-level overview which frames how the 11 use cases combine into the three end-to-end scenarios, cross-references the relevant regulations from Chapter 5, and indicates the integration points with WP3 and WP6. The second layer consists of the individual UC specification sections, each authored by the respective UC lead, following the standardised template introduced in Section 3.4. The detailed UC specification sheets are provided in full in Annex A.

**Chapter 5: Current Landscape** presents the findings of the T4.2 stock-taking activity on existing processes and technical implementations. It describes how each sector currently handles the processes that WP4 aims to transform (Section 5.1), examines the state of identity matching and identity management across the three sectors (Section 5.2), and synthesises the constraints, limitations, and pain points that the WP4 use cases respond to (Section 5.3). This chapter establishes the "before" picture against which the pilot results will later be evaluated in D4.3.

**Chapter 6: Legal and Regulatory Framework** analyses the legal and regulatory context that constrains or enables the WP4 use cases. It first covers the provisions of eIDAS 2.0 and its Implementing Acts that are directly relevant to WP4 (Section 6.1), and then addresses sector-specific regulations (Section 6.2), on hospitality and guest registration, on transport and passenger rights, and on student mobility and academic recognition. 

**Chapter 7: Common Technical Foundations** describes the technical building blocks that are shared across all WP4 use cases, presented once in a common chapter to avoid repetition in each UC specification. It covers EUDIW architecture alignment with the ARF (Section 7.1), the credential types and attestations used across the pilot (Section 7.2), issuance, verification, and revocation flows (Section 7.3), selective disclosure and user consent mechanisms (Section 7.4), the trust framework and trusted issuer lists (Section 7.5), and the standards and protocols that underpin the implementations (Section 7.6). For each topic, the chapter presents a consolidated overview followed by UC-specific subsections.

**Chapter 8: Challenges and Opportunities** steps back from the specifications to reflect on what may go wrong, what is uncertain, and what lies ahead. It lists the risks identified during the stock-taking phase together with the agreed mitigation measures (Section 8.1), and identifies open questions and topics that are deliberately left out of scope for D4.1 but that will need to be addressed in later project phases or in follow-on initiatives (Section 8.2). This chapter is written with the European Commission and the project stakeholders in mind, offering an honest assessment rather than an uncritical showcase.

**Chapter 9: Conclusions and Next Steps** closes the deliverable with a forward-looking perspective. Section 9.1 recaps the 11 specified use cases across the three scenarios and the key findings of the stock-taking. Section 9.2 lists open items that require resolution before or during the implementation phase. Section 9.3 sketches the roadmap towards D4.2, describing the planned activities for T4.3 and the expected structure of the implementation manual. Section 10.4 does the same for D4.3, describing the testing approach, the KPI framework, and the feedback collection mechanisms that will support pilot validation. Milestones 15 (M18) and 16 (M22) are highlighted as the formal checkpoints.

**Annexes**

The annexes collect the detailed material that supports the main body of the deliverable:

* **Annex A (Detailed Use Case Specification Sheets)** provides one full specification sheet per use case, each following the standardised template introduced in Section 3.4. Annex A is the definitive reference for implementation teams working on D4.2.
* **Annex B (Glossary)** contains the full list of terms used throughout the document, expanding on the key definitions introduced in Section 2.5.
* **Annex C (References)** lists the Grant Agreement, the ARF versions referenced, the eIDAS Regulation and its Implementing Acts, the sector-specific standards and regulations, the outputs of WP2, and the other primary sources cited in the document.
