### UC 1: Board the flight (Amadeus)
## Selective Disclosure and User Consent

Selective disclosure and explicit user consent are **legal and architectural requirements** in the *Amadeus Board the Flight* use case. They ensure compliance with **eIDAS 2.0**, applicable **Implementing Acts**, and **GDPR** principles such as data minimisation, purpose limitation, and transparency.

A key design principle of the **EUDI Wallet** is that **selective disclosure and user consent are handled together on a single user screen**, while remaining **clearly distinct from wallet authentication**. These are **two different user actions with different legal meanings**.



It is important to clearly distinguish between:

### 1. Wallet Authentication (Security Action)
- Performed using **PIN**, **biometric unlock**, or equivalent device‑level mechanism.
- Purpose:
  - Authenticate the wallet holder
  - Protect access to wallet contents
- Legal nature:
  - **Security control**
  - Does **not** authorise data sharing
- Effect:
  - Unlocks the wallet
  - Does **not** grant permission to disclose any credential

### 2. Consent & Selective Disclosure (Legal Authorisation)
- Performed through an **explicit approval action** (tap / confirm) on the wallet UI.
- Purpose:
  - Authorise disclosure of **specific data**
  - For a **clearly stated purpose**
- Legal nature:
  - **Explicit user consent** under GDPR and eIDAS 2.0
- Effect:
  - Allows issuance of a **verifiable presentation**
  - Scoped to a **single transaction**

> Unlocking the wallet is **not** consent.  
> Consent is granted only when the user explicitly approves disclosure on the wallet’s consent screen.

## Single‑Screen Consent and Selective Disclosure

For every *Board the Flight* interaction, the EUDI Wallet displays **one consolidated screen** that combines:

- Identity of the requesting relying party
- Purpose of the request (e.g. API collection, boarding verification)
- Exact data elements to be disclosed (e.g. DG1, DG2)
- A clear **approve / deny** action

On this same screen:
- **Selective disclosure** defines *what data* will be shared
- **Consent** authorises *that disclosure for that purpose*

Both are confirmed by **one explicit affirmative action**, fully transparent to the user.

## Episode 1 – Collect and Verify API at Self‑Service Online Check‑In (SSCI)

**Context:** Online, remote  
**Credential example:** Digital Travel Credential (DTC)  
**Data disclosed:** **DG1**

- The traveler authenticates to the wallet (PIN / biometric).
- The wallet presents **one consent screen** showing:
  - Airline / Amadeus system identity
  - Purpose: *Advance Passenger Information collection*
  - Full **DG1** data set
- The traveler explicitly approves disclosure.
- The wallet produces a verifiable presentation containing **DG1 only**.

No biometric data is requested or disclosed in this episode.


### DG1 Data Elements (MRZ‑Derived)

The DG1 dataset includes the full MRZ content, typically comprising:

- **Document type**
- **Issuing State or Organisation**
- **Document number**
- **Document number check digit**
- **Primary identifier** (surname / family name)
- **Secondary identifier(s)** (given name(s))
- **Nationality**
- **Date of birth**
- **Date of birth check digit**
- **Sex**
- **Date of expiry**
- **Date of expiry check digit**
- **Optional data field(s)** (national optional elements, document‑specific)
- **Composite check digit**



### Characteristics

- DG1 reflects **exactly the data encoded in the MRZ**, including filler characters where applicable.
- The dataset is **digitally signed by the issuing authority**, enabling integrity and authenticity verification via trust anchors.
- DG1 is suitable for:
  - **Advance Passenger Information (API)**



The **Security Object Document (SOD)** is a signed data structure issued by the passport‑issuing authority. It provides cryptographic protection for data groups stored in the LDS, including DG1.

The SOD contains:
- A **digital signature** created with the **Document Signer (DS)** private key
- **Cryptographic hashes** for each protected data group (including DG1)
- The **Document Signer Certificate**, chaining up to the national **CSCA**


SOD verification may be applied **optionally**, depending on assurance level. SOD verification is not mandatory in current API workflows

## Episode 2 – Board the Flight at the Airport

**Context:** On‑site, proximity‑based  
**Credential example:** Digital Travel Credential (DTC)  
**Data disclosed:** **DG1 + DG2**

- The traveler authenticates to the wallet (PIN / biometric).
- The wallet displays **one combined consent & disclosure screen** showing:
  - Boarding system identity
  - Purpose: *boarding verification*
  - Requested data: **DG1** and **DG2**
- The traveler explicitly approves.
- The wallet shares DG1 and DG2 for:
  - Identity verification
  - Optional **one‑off 1:1 biometric match**

DG2 is used transiently and never stored beyond the transaction.


SOD verification may be applied **optionally**, depending on assurance level. SOD verification is not mandatory in current boart the flight workflows. 




For **border‑control‑related use cases**, as specified in **WP3**, the situation is different:

- **SOD verification is mandatory** for any border control scenario.
- This applies **regardless of channel**, including:
  - **Online pre‑screening or pre‑arrival flows**
  - **Physical border crossing at checkpoints**
- Border authorities must always:
  - Verify DG1 (and DG2 where applicable) **against the SOD**
  - Validate the issuing authority’s trust chain
  - Ensure full passive authentication compliance



## Key Principles

- **Authentication ≠ Consent**  
  Unlocking the wallet does not authorise data sharing.
- **Single‑screen transparency**  
  Consent and selective disclosure are reviewed together.
- **Explicit affirmative action required**  
  No background, silent, or implicit disclosure is allowed.
- **Episode‑specific scope**  
  Consent in Episode 1 does not carry over to Episode 2.
- **User sovereignty by design**  
  The traveler always controls *when*, *why*, and *what* data is shared.
- **SOD verification is optional**  
  SOD verification improves integrity and authenticity, however from this use case perspective, **SOD may be applied optionally**

> By separating **wallet authentication** from **consent and selective disclosure**, while presenting consent and disclosure together on one clear user screen, the *Amadeus Board the Flight* use case achieves strong security, legal clarity, and privacy‑by‑design.
