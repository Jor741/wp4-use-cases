## Current Constraints, Limitations and Pain Points

### UC1

- **Added end‑user friction on smartphone**
  - During boarding with **EUDIW**, the traveler must:
    - Review and accept a **selective disclosure request**.
    - Explicitly provide consent for data sharing.
    - **Authenticate on the phone** (PIN or biometrics) to enable a **1:1 identity match** before sending data.
  - These steps are new compared to traditional boarding flows.

- **Redundancy of identity checks**
  - The **1:1 match performed on the smartphone** is often followed by:
    - A **second 1:1 face match at the boarding gate** (eGate or agent‑assisted).
  - From the traveler’s perspective, this duplication is hard to justify and perceived as redundant.

- **User experience degradation**
  - The accumulation of consent screens, authentication prompts, and repeated face checks:
    - Breaks the “passive” boarding experience users are accustomed to.
    - Contradicts expectations of speed and simplicity.
  - Instead of feeling frictionless, digital identity risks being perceived as **more complex than showing a physical ID**.

- **Adoption risk**
  - Travelers may not understand:
    - Why multiple identity confirmations are needed.
    - Why digital identity feels more demanding than manual checks.
  - Without clear and immediate benefits (faster boarding, fewer stops), this **new friction threatens user adoption**.

### UC2

The implementation of Electronic Travel Authorizations (ETAs) has created challenges, including erroneous boarding denials, confusion over requirements, and technical glitches for travelers. Key issues include inconsistent enforcement by airlines, lack of appeal rights for refusals, and difficulties for residents with existing immigration permissions. UC2 does not have the ambition to solve all these challenges. Nevertheless, we will be happy if we are able to solve a few of them by reducing misinterpretation of ETAs via harmonization and by offering increased usability and transparency via improved user control.

UC2 specific constraints include the willingness for national authorities to accept standardized ETAs issued by an external eIDAS QEAA provider.

### UC3

A key constraint in UC3 is that the traveller journey spans multiple domains — such as airport, hospitality, ferry transport, and university-related services — but these domains do not yet operate on a single shared identity and process layer. Identity verification, booking validation, entitlement checks, and access decisions are still handled through separate sector-specific systems, which creates fragmentation across the journey.

Another important limitation is that the intended wallet-based flow depends on the integration of several operational systems that were not originally designed to work together through a common credential-presentation model. This means that even where wallet-based verification is technically feasible, the surrounding operational processes may still require adaptation, fallback handling, or partial manual support.

In the airport-related flow, an additional pain point is the potential accumulation of verification steps. Wallet-based presentation, consent, and authentication may improve assurance and privacy, but they can also introduce extra interaction on the traveller’s device. If these steps are followed by further checkpoint-side validation or biometric comparison, the overall experience may be perceived as repetitive or more demanding than current practice unless the user benefit is clear.

In the hospitality flow, a major limitation is the heterogeneity of hotel environments. Some hotels may support modern PMS integration and partial automation, while others still depend on manual booking lookup, reception-assisted identification, or loosely coupled booking processes. As a result, the pilot cannot assume a uniform self-service implementation across all participating sites.

More generally, UC3 is constrained by the fact that full end-to-end automation is not yet realistic in every episode. The pilot must therefore accommodate a mix of implementation models, ranging from more integrated wallet-based flows to semi-assisted or fallback variants. This is not only a technical issue, but also an operational one, since service providers need solutions that fit into existing processes without disrupting service continuity.

Overall, the main constraints in UC3 are therefore:
- fragmentation across sector-specific systems and operational domains;
- uneven technical maturity and integration readiness among participating service providers;
- the need to support both automated and assisted process variants;
- potential duplication of verification steps from the traveller’s perspective; and
- the risk that a technically strong solution may still face adoption challenges if the user experience is not sufficiently seamless.

### UC4

A main constraint in UC4 is that airport access cannot really be issued fully remotely. Identity checks and background screening still require physical presence or a controlled onboarding step, which limits how early access can be prepared.

Because of that, employees and visitors often only get access once they are already on-site, or they need to be escorted until everything is cleared. This makes timing quite tight, especially for short notice visits.

Another limitation is that access rights depend on security processes that are not very flexible. If something changes last minute, it usually means manual updates or coordination with security or the host.

So in practice, identity checks, clearance, and access provisioning are still closely tied together in operational processes, which makes it harder to handle access in a more digital or pre-arranged way.


### UC5
One of the main contraints in UC 5 is that the system is already live, so changes to identity, ticketing or access cannot be introduced all at once without affecting current stadium operations.

Identity, ticketing and access are still split across different systems. Because of that, moving everything into a single wallet flow is not straightforward.

It is also not always easy to add new types of users, like interns or student-related roles, because access rules are tied to existing club and ticketing logic.

So the main pain point is not the lack of digital access, but the fact that everything already works in a live setup, and changes need to fit into that without breaking what is already in place.


### UC6

Current approaches to identity matching and student status verification are fragmented, inconsistent, and often limited to national or institutional contexts. Students frequently rely on **physical cards or non-standardised digital credentials**, which are not always recognised across borders or by different service providers. This leads to manual verification processes, increased friction, and limited access to benefits.
Operationally, organisations must manage heterogeneous identity systems and adapt to new trust frameworks introduced by the EUDIW. This includes onboarding wallet-compatible infrastructure, aligning with **eIDAS 2.0 requirements**, and ensuring acceptance of credentials issued by diverse providers.
Pilot implementations will therefore need to operate within these constraints, addressing interoperability gaps, supporting gradual adoption, and ensuring compatibility with both legacy systems and emerging EUDIW standards.

### UC7-UC8

Considering the actual absence of any identity matching on UC7 and UC8 real processes, PagoPA and Trenitalia need to re-design the scenario including the Wallet interactions in their actual processes.
In order to avoid difficulties or critical issues in the production systems and consequences on Italian real users, UC7 and UC8 will be piloted in  pre-production using fake mock data. PagoPA will develop a Test App (PagoPA EUDIW), in which testers can try the experience of presenting their credentials via that EUDIW to the Trenitalia app, which will verify their status and deliver the service.

### UC9

The current solution relying on a 3rd PVID party provider and requires traveler to perform many steps like manually entering data, scanning their id document and performing face biometric and liveness check in order to requests their biometric profile and ready to travel attestation. This system adds friction to the process and some traveller can abandoned it without completion because it is too long or tedious.
