## Current Processes and Existing Implementations (All UC leads)

### UC1
The *Board the Flight* use case has been **tested within EWC (European Wallet Consortium)**. Previoius test was conducted using **mocked data** and a **testing version of the EUDIW (EU Digital Identity Wallet)**.

  The existing online process (e.g. online check-in / pre‑travel steps) can technically integrate EUDIW‑based verification.
  However, it **requires adaptation** for end users to accept and understand this new way of verifying their digital identity.
  The flow represents a shift from traditional document handling to **user‑controlled credential sharing**.
Adoption **cannot rely on user guides or instructions**.

The experience must be **Intuitive and self‑explanatory** : seamlessly embedded in existing check‑in and user journeys

 **Design principle**
  - Acceptance of EUDIW‑based verification must be driven by **tangible user value**
  - Digital identity should feel like a **natural enhancement** of the journey, not an additional step to learn or configure.

### UC2

Currently several countries have implemented their own Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) process. This proposed ETA use case tries to standardise these different processes into a single harmonised EUDIW-based process that leverages the existing eIDAS trust infrastructure and that offers travellers more control over their ETA credentials and a more friendly user experience. 

### UC3

The **SEDIT-X** use case builds on existing operational processes across **airport, ferry transport, hospitality, and university-related travel contexts**, but introduces the **EUDI Wallet** as a common user-controlled interaction channel across these domains.

In the current baseline, these processes are still largely **fragmented**. Identity checks, travel entitlements, booking confirmations, and access decisions are typically handled through separate sector-specific systems, often requiring repeated presentation of documents or repeated manual data entry. The current traveller journey is therefore not yet based on a single interoperable wallet-driven flow. 

For the **airport episode**, the uploaded material shows that the current implementation baseline is not limited to online check-in. Rather, it is evolving toward a broader **smart-airport** approach in which the wallet may be used across multiple operational touchpoints, including **check-in, baggage drop-off, security screening, and boarding**. Two implementation paths are being examined:  
- a **biometric QR code** model, where the passenger presents a secure QR-based airport journey token; and  
- a **proximity verification** model, where the passenger presents wallet-held credentials through **NFC / ISO 18013-5-style proximity interaction**.  
In both cases, the solution requires integration with airport and airline systems and may involve **1:1 biometric verification** at selected checkpoints. 

The implementation path relies on an **EUDIW Intermediary Service**. This intermediary acts as the common integration layer between wallet protocols and operational systems, rather than requiring each airport touchpoint to implement full wallet logic independently. In this sense, the current implementation direction is toward a **shared wallet integration layer** rather than isolated verifier deployments per checkpoint. 

For the **hospitality episode**, the current process remains close to the existing booking and hotel check-in model. The booking portal, DMC, and hotel-side processes remain largely unchanged in business terms, while wallet-based interaction is introduced mainly to:  
- collect verified traveller identity data during booking, and  
- issue a **Booking Reference Credential** that can later be used during hotel check-in.  
Next, at hotel arrival, the EUDI Wallet enables  a **self-service or semi-assisted check-in flow**,

Overall, the current state of SEDIT-X should therefore be described as a **progressive transition** from fragmented, document-heavy, and sector-specific processes toward a more interoperable wallet-based journey. The pilot does not assume that all participating systems are already fully integrated. Instead, it builds on existing operational processes and introduces wallet-based issuance, verification, and intermediary-supported integration where feasible in each episode.  

### UC4
Today, airport access for employees is based on physical access badges issued by the airport or its security operators. Employees do have an access pass, but it is usually limited to specific zones, and not always sufficient on its own. If they need to go into other areas, they often need to be accompanied by someone with the right access to open doors.

Visitors are even more restricted. They are registered in advance, but they do not always receive a dedicated visitor badge. In many cases access is handled through escorting by an authorised employee.

Identity checks are still done in a fairly traditional way during registration, followed in some cases by issuing a physical badge or a QR-based pass. The different systems for identity, visitor management, and physical access control are not always fully connected and often work separately from each other.

So access today is still very much based on local rules and escorting, rather than a unified digital identity-based access flow.


### UC5
Todays stadium access is already live and used in a real operational environment.

Fans first buy a ticket through the club’s existing ticketing system. After that, they use the FastID wallet for identity verification and to link their profile to the club (N.E.C. account), but this is mainly used for account and identity purposes, not for direct stadium entry.

The actual access credential for entering the stadium is still usually handled through the club’s ticketing system or a separate app. At the gate, the ticket is checked from that system before entry is allowed.

At the stadium entrance, access is granted after ticket validation, and in some cases biometric verification is used to speed up or confirm the identity of the person entering.

Employees also use the same setup for access to staff areas, including interns or temporary staff, based on their assigned rights.

So today the system is already digital and operational, but identity, ticketing and access are still split across different systems. The wallet is not yet the single place where the access credential is shown at the gate, even though this is the direction the solution is moving toward.


### UC6

In **France**, the current processes and infrastructure already provide a strong foundation for the implementation of our scenario of the student card within EUDIW:
- A national **wallet infrastructure is currently under development**, building on existing digital identity systems. While most components are technically ready, the remaining effort focuses on aligning with the ARF standard to enable full production deployment.
- In parallel, partial registries for student cards already exist and are operated by publics and private providers; these include digital versions of student credentials that will need to be reissued or adapted to comply with EUDIW protocols, allowing their integration into the wallet ecosystem. 
- From a technological perspective, providers already have core systems in place, but additional work is required to develop connectors between registries and the EUDI Wallet to support credential issuance in a standardized way. 
- On the relying party side, service providers will need to implement **OID4VP protocols** to ensure they can securely request and receive verifiable data from the wallet.

In **Italy**, various stakeholders are working toward full alignment with the ARF infrastructure and the broader Wallet Ecosystem. 
- Primarily, **PagoPA** already provides a National Public Digital Wallet, known as **IT Wallet**, which allows users to digitize several personal documents. While its infrastructure is being evolved to achieve full compliance with eIDAS 2.0 requirements and the ARF, its current functionalities are already closely aligned with these regulatory standards. For UC6, PagoPA will utilize a dedicated testing application, **PagoPA EUDIW**; this is a high-fidelity reproduction of the national wallet which operates in a pre-production system with mock data and it is ideated  to facilitate the design, development, and testing of the scenario without interfering with production systems.
- Regarding the issuance process, **Istituto Poligrafico e Zecca dello Stato** (IPZS) will act as the official PID provider, directly contributing to the scenario by issuing the necessary credential. 
- On the academic side, **Polimi** will issue the Student Card. Although Polimi students can already upgrade their cards to the European Student Card format to access benefits during Erasmus programs, UC6 will enhance this by making the credential fully interoperable. This ensures that students can seamlessly access services in other member states, such as France, through a standardized European framework.

### UC7-UC8
Nowadays, passengers with specific conditions need to verify their status manually, through certificates in different formats and mostly requiring support. 
The integration with EUDIW will streamline the process, enabling users to access discounts and personalized journeys by only using their EUDIW, which renders the journey more accessible to everyone. 
UC7 and UC8 will rely on the Italian digital Wallet infrastructure, which is nowadays working to be fully aligned with the eIDAS 2.0 regulation and ARF. More specifically, PagoPA and Trenitalia will pilot this UC using a Test Wallet App (PagoPA EUDIW), provided by PagoPA, equivalent to the italian wallet (IT Wallet), which allows to test the use cases, without interfering with the production systems. The objective is to develop, implement and test these Use Cases in a pre-production boundary, in order to prepare the framework for the production whenever this will be possible after the pilot. Although referring to mock data for the credentials, the process will be a real simulation as it will work in future production systems.

### UC9

Currently, passengers requesting their biometric profile or ready to travel attestation need to verify their identity manually using a Remote Proof of Identity and Liveness Verification (PVID). The integration with EUDIW will streamline the process, enabling users to access authenticate only using their EUDIW, which ease the journey for everyone. The onboarding system connecting the identity verification process and the attestation issuance to the Marseille App are already in place but the systems need to adapt to acquiring attributes from a EUDIW as well as the solution mechanism allowing traveller to present their biometric profile and ready to travel attestations in the Marseille Airport. UC9 will rely on the French digital Wallet infrastructure, which is nowadays working to be fully aligned with the eIDAS 2.0 regulation and ARF. Traveller will be able to easily share their PID and DTC attributes to the verifier. 

### UC 11

A French student moving to Quebec today, typically under the France–Quebec cooperation arrangement on tuition (Entente France–Québec), completes a document-heavy, multi-step process involving French-side procedures, Canadian federal procedures (IRCC), Quebec provincial procedures (MIFI), and institution-specific onboarding processes.

This typically includes:
- admission to the institution, with academic records submitted as uploaded documentary evidence or certified copies;
- issuance of a Certificat d’acceptation du Québec (CAQ) by MIFI;
- application for a Canadian study permit through IRCC, relying on uploaded supporting documentation;
- and, upon arrival, in-person identity and document verification at the institution, which then issues a local student card used for many downstream interactions (e.g. library access, campus facilities, and transit discounts).

Student housing operates through a mix of on-campus residences (typically requiring proof of admission and government-issued identification, often processed manually) and private providers accepting a broader and less standardized set of documents.

Public-transport student discounts (e.g. STM, RTC, STL) are generally tied to institutionally validated student status and local student-card workflows, and do not directly recognise the French GYSC credential today.

Existing digital infrastructure on the EU side includes the EUDIW Reference Implementation as well as French PID and GYSC issuance systems. On the Canadian side, no broadly deployed verifier infrastructure interoperable with EU-issued EUDI Wallet credentials appears to be in operational use across higher-education and student-service ecosystems today.
