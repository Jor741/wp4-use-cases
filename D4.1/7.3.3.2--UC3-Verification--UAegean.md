#### Verification


SEDIT-X uses the EUDI Wallet as the common presentation channel across its transport and hospitality episodes, while keeping the verification logic aligned with standard wallet presentation patterns. At a general level, verification follows an **OpenID4VP-style flow**: a relying party or verifier initiates a request, the request is delivered to the traveller’s wallet through a supported engagement method, the wallet shows the requested credentials and disclosure purpose, the traveller approves, and the verifier validates issuer trust, signature integrity, credential validity and status information, policy satisfaction, and the sufficiency of the disclosed attributes before returning a decision to the operational system. In SEDIT-X, this common pattern is reused across episodes, but each domain applies additional checks that are specific to its operational context, such as flight-validity checks, reservation lookup, or boarding entitlement checks. The **EUDIW Intermediary Service** acts as the main integration layer between wallet protocols and domain-specific back-end systems, especially in the airport, ferry, and hotel episodes. 

Across SEDIT-X, the verifier’s task is not limited to checking a credential signature. Verification also includes confirming that the presented evidence is valid **for the current transaction context**. This means, depending on the episode, checking trust anchors, credential status or revocation information, binding between related credentials, freshness and replay protections, and domain-specific eligibility conditions. This is particularly visible in the airport and hotel episodes, where the presentation received from the wallet must be connected to live operational data such as a valid flight segment, a reservation record, or a hotel check-in state.  

#### Common Verification Pattern Across SEDIT-X

The common verification pattern in SEDIT-X can be described as follows:

1. A service provider system acting as verifier initiates a request for specific credentials or attributes.
2. The request is conveyed to the traveller’s EUDI Wallet through an appropriate engagement method, such as QR-based engagement, deep link, redirect, or proximity interaction.
3. The wallet presents to the traveller the requested credentials, the purpose of the request, and the minimum data to be disclosed.
4. The traveller authenticates locally to the wallet and consents to disclosure.
5. The wallet constructs a verifiable presentation and returns it to the verifier, typically via the EUDIW Intermediary Service.
6. The verifier checks cryptographic integrity, issuer trust, validity period, credential status, and whether the presentation satisfies the operational policy for the transaction.
7. The verifier returns a decision to the operational system, which either allows the traveller to proceed or triggers fallback or staff-assisted handling.

This common structure gives SEDIT-X a consistent user and verifier model across domains, while still allowing each episode to request different credentials and perform different back-end checks. 

#### Airport Verification Flows

The airport episode contains the most complex verification logic in SEDIT-X. It supports two parallel verification patterns at security screening and gate boarding:

- a **BioQRCode flow**, where the traveller presents a previously issued airport biometric QR credential, and
- a **proximity verification flow**, where the traveller presents a live verifiable presentation of the **PID** and **Boarding Pass Credential** from the wallet, for example through NFC-style proximity interaction. 

At the **security checkpoint**, the traveller approaches the checkpoint and is prompted to present the EUDI Wallet. In the BioQRCode variant, the traveller unlocks and displays the previously issued biometric QR credential for scanning by a local reader. In the proximity variant, the traveller activates the wallet’s tap-based presentation flow, after which the EUDIW Intermediary Service requests the Boarding Pass Credential and the PID, including a minimal identity attribute set for binding and, where biometric-enhanced mode is active, the portrait image from the PID. The wallet displays a consent screen, the traveller approves, and the wallet constructs the presentation.  

Once the evidence is received, the **EUDIW Intermediary Service** performs the back-end verification steps. First, it validates authenticity and integrity, including issuer signatures, trust anchors, validity periods, and status or revocation information. Then it performs **journey-context validation**. In the BioQRCode path, the service confirms that the biometric QR credential corresponds to the traveller’s current journey context and checks through airport and airline operational interfaces that the traveller is eligible for the specific security-screening step on that date and for the relevant flight. In the proximity path, it confirms that the Boarding Pass Credential subject matches the PID subject, and then checks against airport and airline operational systems that the traveller is eligible for that flight and that checkpoint step. If these checks fail, the process is rejected and the traveller is redirected to staff-assisted handling. If they succeed, the traveller may proceed directly in credential-only mode, or continue to biometric verification in biometric-enhanced mode.  

Where biometric-enhanced mode is used, the checkpoint triggers a **one-off live facial capture**. The biometric comparison remains a **1:1 verification**, not a 1:N identification process. In the BioQRCode flow, the live capture is compared against the protected biometric reference associated with the biometric QR credential. In the proximity flow, the live capture is compared against the portrait image disclosed from the PID according to the pilot assumption. If the match is above threshold, the traveller is allowed to proceed and the live and reference data are discarded from memory. If the match fails or confidence is too low, the automated flow stops and staff-assisted handling is triggered.  

At the **boarding gate**, the logic is similar but tied to final boarding eligibility. The gate asks the traveller to present the EUDI Wallet. In the proximity variant, the EUDIW Intermediary Service requests the Boarding Pass Credential and PID with the minimal identity attributes required for binding and, where biometric-enhanced mode is active, the portrait image from the PID for one-off 1:1 matching. The wallet presents the consent screen, the traveller approves, and the presentation is sent back. The verifier then checks signatures, trust anchors, and revocation or status information for the credentials, confirms that the Boarding Pass Credential subject matches the PID subject, and confirms through the airline’s operational systems that the boarding pass is valid and boarding is currently open for that flight. If the checks succeed, the system may proceed to live capture and 1:1 biometric comparison before allowing boarding. If the checks fail, the traveller is redirected to staff-assisted handling. 

Overall, the airport verification flow demonstrates the strongest SEDIT-X pattern of **multi-layer verification**: cryptographic credential validation, subject binding between credentials, operational eligibility validation through airline and airport back ends, and optional one-off biometric matching at the point of control. 

#### Hotel Verification Flows

The hotel episode applies the same wallet verification pattern in two places: during booking-time identification and during on-site self-service check-in. The hotel working paper explicitly frames these interactions as standard **OpenID4VP 1.0-style verification/presentation flows**, implemented through the **EUDIW Intermediary Service**, while the issuance of the Booking Reference Credential is handled separately through OpenID4VCI. The hotel-side relying party may therefore be either the **booking portal** during booking-time identification or the **self-service check-in terminal** during hotel arrival.  

During **booking-time verification**, the user selects digital identification through the EUDI Wallet in the booking portal. The booking portal initiates a presentation flow via the EUDIW Intermediary Service, which generates the presentation request. The traveller receives the request in the wallet, consents to the disclosure of the requested identity attributes, and the Intermediary Service validates the response and returns the verified identity attributes, or an equivalent verified result set, to the booking portal. The booking process itself remains largely unchanged from the business point of view; the difference is that the traveller’s identity data are gathered directly and accurately from the wallet rather than being entered manually. 

During **self-service hotel check-in**, the traveller arrives at the hotel and starts the process from a tablet or similar self-service terminal. The terminal initiates a verification session through the EUDIW Intermediary Service, which generates a presentation request asking for the personal identification attributes required under the hotel’s jurisdiction together with the **Booking Reference Credential**. The traveller receives the request in the wallet and consents to disclosure. The self-service system then receives the verified identity data through the Intermediary Service and uses the booking reference carried in the Booking Reference Credential to identify the reservation. Where the hotel’s technical environment permits it, the system may then retrieve the booking from the PMS, update guest details, and update the check-in state.  

The hotel verification flow is therefore centred on three checks: first, that the presented identity evidence is trusted and valid; second, that the presented Booking Reference Credential is valid and usable for reservation lookup; and third, that the disclosed identity data and booking reference are sufficient for the hotel’s operational and legal check-in requirements. Unlike the airport flow, the hotel flow does not add biometric verification. Its complexity instead lies in the variability of hotel IT environments. The same verification outcome may feed into different deployment variants, ranging from reception-assisted verification, to receipt-based assisted flow, to partial PMS integration, and eventually to a fully integrated self-service model. 

####  Ferry Verification Flow

The ferry episode follows the same architectural approach as the other SEDIT-X transport domains, although the currently available WP4 material describes it at a higher level than the airport and hotel episodes. In this episode, the traveller uses the wallet as the common presentation channel for maritime travel entitlements and related identity evidence, with the **EUDIW Intermediary Service** again acting as the integration layer between wallet protocols and operator-side booking, ticketing, and boarding systems. At booking time, the ferry operator or participating travel agency issues a **Ferry Boarding Credential** to the traveller’s wallet. Where applicable, additional wallet-held credentials such as **student status**, **accessibility/disability attestations**, and potentially an **mVRC** can support entitlement validation or special travel conditions.  

At boarding time, the traveller presents the required wallet-based evidence through a proximity-style interaction. The ferry-side verifier checks the validity of the Ferry Boarding Credential and, where needed, additional evidence such as identity data, discount entitlements, or vehicle-related credentials. The purpose of the ferry verification flow is to confirm that the traveller is entitled to board, that any claimed discount or special condition is backed by a verifiable credential, and that the presentation satisfies the boarding policy for the specific sailing. In the current SEDIT-X framing, the ferry flow is conceptually aligned with the same common verification pattern used elsewhere, but without the additional biometric layer that appears in the airport episode. 

####  Verification Logic Across the Use Case

Taken together, the SEDIT-X verification flows show a common technical logic across domains:

- the traveller remains in control of disclosure through the wallet;
- the verifier requests only the credentials or attributes needed for the current step;
- the EUDIW Intermediary Service handles wallet-facing technical complexity and bridges to domain systems;
- cryptographic validation is combined with **transaction-context validation**;
- each domain adds its own eligibility logic, such as flight validity, reservation lookup, or boarding entitlement;
- when automation fails or system integration is limited, the process falls back to staff-assisted handling rather than blocking the journey entirely.

This makes verification in SEDIT-X not just a credential-checking function, but an **operational decision process** that combines trusted digital evidence from the wallet with live domain-system state.
