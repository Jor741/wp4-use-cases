## UC 3: Seamless Digital Traveller Experience / SEDIT-X (UAegean)

The **Seamless Digital Traveller Experience (SEDIT-X)** use case demonstrates how the **EUDI Wallet** can support a continuous, cross-domain, and privacy-preserving traveller journey spanning **air transport, urban mobility, ferry transport, hospitality, and academic access**. In the context of WP4, the objective of the use case is to show that the wallet can act as a **common interaction channel** across heterogeneous service environments, enabling travellers to rely on a consistent wallet-based experience rather than fragmented, repeatedly manual, or document-heavy processes.

SEDIT-X is designed as an **end-to-end journey scenario** rather than as a single isolated service flow. It starts from the traveller’s airport experience, continues through local mobility and onward ferry travel, extends to hotel booking and check-in, and culminates in access to selected university services. In this way, the use case explores how the same wallet-based logic can be reused across multiple operational domains: credentials are issued into the wallet by trusted parties, selectively presented by the traveller when needed, verified by service-side systems or intermediary-enabled verifiers, and then used to support context-specific operational decisions.

At a business level, SEDIT-X responds to a common challenge across the travel ecosystem: the traveller is repeatedly asked to prove identity, entitlement, booking status, eligibility, or payment authorisation at different points of the journey, often through disconnected systems and with limited interoperability between sectors. The use case therefore examines how the EUDI Wallet can reduce friction, administrative burden, and repeated data handling by enabling **selective disclosure**, **trusted digital verification**, and a more seamless progression across service touchpoints.

At an architectural level, SEDIT-X is based on the assumption that domain systems such as airport infrastructure, ferry booking and boarding environments, hotel booking and PMS environments, and university access services do not need to become wallet-native in the same way. Instead, the wallet acts as the **user-facing presentation and consent channel**, while the service-side environment consumes the verified outcome through the relevant verification or integration flow. In several episodes, this logic is supported by an **EUDIW Intermediary Service**, which helps bridge wallet protocols and sector-specific operational systems.

The use case also serves as a practical demonstration of how the wallet may support not only identity verification, but also the broader orchestration of **travel, entitlement, and payment-related interactions**. Depending on the episode, the credentials and evidence involved may include the **Person Identification Data (PID)** credential, a **boarding-pass credential**, a **biometric QR or equivalent airport journey token**, a **ferry boarding credential**, **student or accessibility-related attestations**, a **booking reference credential** for hotel flows, and, where relevant, **payment-related evidence** or service-specific access credentials. The exact credential set varies by episode, but the underlying principle remains the same: only the minimum data needed for the specific operational decision should be disclosed.

A further objective of SEDIT-X is to show that wallet-enabled service continuity can be applied in a way that remains compatible with real-world infrastructure constraints. For this reason, the use case intentionally includes episodes with different levels of technical maturity and integration readiness. Some episodes, such as airport biometric boarding, explore more advanced verification patterns. Others, such as urban mobility and academic access, remain more targeted or high-level at the present stage of specification. Likewise, in hospitality, the pilot explicitly accommodates multiple implementation variants depending on the maturity of the participating hotel environment. This flexibility reflects the reality of cross-sector piloting and allows the use case to remain grounded in feasible deployment paths.

The SEDIT-X scenario is also intended to reflect an **inclusive-by-design** approach. In addition to the general traveller journey, the use case considers situations in which the traveller may rely on other wallet-held credentials, such as student-status or accessibility-related attestations, in order to support verified entitlements or more appropriate service delivery. This helps position SEDIT-X not only as a technical interoperability exercise, but also as a user-centric exploration of how the wallet can support fairer and more context-aware service interactions across borders.

Within this deliverable, UC3 is therefore presented as a **composite use case** organised into five connected episodes:

1. **Episode 1 – Smart Airport: Biometric Boarding**  
   Demonstrates wallet-supported identity and boarding-entitlement verification across airport touchpoints, including a biometric 1:1 verification variant without pre-enrolment.

2. **Episode 2 – Urban Mobility in Athens**  
   Explores the use of the wallet for a targeted local mobility payment interaction, currently focused on taxi payment in Athens and aligned with the payment-related work developed in WP6.

3. **Episode 3 – Ferry Transport**  
   Examines how wallet-held travel and identity credentials can support ferry booking, entitlement validation, discount handling, and proximity-based boarding.

4. **Episode 4 – Hospitality**  
   Explores the use of the wallet in hotel booking and self-service or assisted check-in flows, including the issuance and later presentation of a booking reference credential.

5. **Episode 5 – Academic Access**  
   Extends the SEDIT-X journey into the higher-education domain by examining how wallet-based interactions can support access to selected services offered by the University of the Aegean to ERUA participants.

Taken together, these five episodes illustrate how the EUDI Wallet may support a **seamless digital traveller experience** across multiple sectors while preserving user control, minimising unnecessary data sharing, and enabling interoperable verification outcomes.
