#### Trusted Issuer Lists
##### Trusted Issuer Lists — Amadeus *Board the Flight*

###### Purpose

In the *Amadeus Board the Flight* use case, relying parties must be able to determine **whether the issuer of a presented credential is trusted** for the purposes of boarding‑related verification.

The **Trusted Issuer List** provides the issuer‑recognition component of the trust framework.  
It answers one specific question:

> *Is this issuer trusted to issue this type of credential for the Board the Flight use case?*


In the **EUDI Wallet / eIDAS 2.0 ecosystem**, the terms **trusted issuer**, **certified issuer**, and **qualified issuer** refer to **different sources and levels of trust**. They are often confused but have **distinct legal meanings and architectural implications**.  
This distinction is essential for correctly designing and operating APTITUDE and *Amadeus Board the Flight* use cases.


##### Trusted Issuer

**Definition**

A **trusted issuer** is an issuer that a relying party recognises as acceptable **within a defined trust framework or governance context**.

Trust is established through:
- inclusion in a **Trusted Issuer List** (e.g. APTITUDE pilot TL),
- governance or regulatory decision (e.g. WP3 rules),
- or recognition by a shared ecosystem (e.g. EUDI Wallet trust model).

**Legal Meaning**

- Trust is **contextual and scoped** (pilot, use case, domain).
- Trust does **not** automatically grant EU‑wide legal effect.
- Trust answers the question:  
  > *“Is this issuer recognised for this use case?”*

**In APTITUDE**

- WP4 relying parties (e.g. Amadeus) rely on an **APTITUDE pilot Trust List**.
- Trust is **required** to proceed with verification.
- Trust alone is **not sufficient** to accept a credential.

**Examples**

- A national authority issuing PID or DTC under WP3.
- An airline trusted in the pilot to issue boarding credentials.
- A university trusted to issue student attestations.

##### Certified Issuer

**Definition**

A **certified issuer** is an issuer (or issuing system) that has passed a **technical or security certification** against defined standards.

Certification typically applies to:
- wallet software,
- issuance platforms,
- security components,
- operational processes.

**Legal Meaning**

- Certification is **technical and procedural**, not sovereign.
- It is granted by an **accredited conformity assessment body**.
- Certification answers:  
  > *“Is this issuer or system compliant with required standards?”*

Certification **does not** automatically:
- make an issuer trusted for a use case,
- grant legal authority to issue identity or travel documents.

**In APTITUDE**

- An issuer may be technically certified
- but still **not included in the pilot Trusted Issuer List**.
- Certification supports **security and interoperability**, not governance.

**Example**

- A wallet or issuance platform certified under eIDAS 2.0 requirements.

##### Qualified Issuer

**Definition**

A **qualified issuer** is a **Qualified Trust Service Provider (QTSP)** providing **qualified trust services** under eIDAS.

Qualification is:
- **defined by law** (eIDAS Regulation),
- **granted by a national supervisory authority**, and
- **published in official EU Trusted Lists (TL / LOTL)**.

**Legal Meaning**

- Qualification has **direct legal effect across the EU**.
- Qualified services benefit from:
  - presumption of legal validity,
  - reversed burden of proof in disputes.
- Qualification applies **only to specific trust services**
  (e.g. qualified signatures, seals, timestamps, QEAA).

##### Comparison Summary

| Aspect | Trusted Issuer | Certified Issuer | Qualified Issuer |
|------|---------------|------------------|------------------|
| Basis | Governance / trust framework | Conformity assessment | eIDAS legal designation |
| Scope | Context‑specific (pilot/use case) | Technical / operational | EU‑wide legal |
| Who defines it | Pilot, WP rules, ecosystem | Certification bodies | National supervisory authority |
| Legal presumption | No | No | Yes |
| Typical example | WP3 DTC issuer | Certified wallet operator | QTSP |

##### Issuer Categories Relevant to *Board the Flight*

For the *Board the Flight* use case, the **Trusted Issuer** is expected to support at least the following issuer categories:

- **PID issuers / national identity authorities**
- **DTC attestation providers** explicitly participating in the pilot 

In the APTITUDE pilot, **WP3** is responsible for use cases related to **Digital Travel Credentials (DTC)** and **border‑control‑grade identity**, including the issuance and revocation of credentials in coordination with **national issuing authorities**.

For these use cases, trusted issuers are **legal and sovereign entities** acting under national law.  

Thus, in this use case, **Trusted Issuer** therefore reflects **WP3 authority and responsibility**, rather than purely commercial or domain‑specific issuers.

****### Use in the Verification Flow
When a credential is presented in *Board the Flight*:

1. Amadeus (or the EUDIW Intermediary Service) checks whether the issuer is listed in the **Trusted Issuer List**.
2. It verifies that the issuer is authorised for the **presented credential type**.
3. If issuer recognition succeeds, Amadeus proceeds with:
   - cryptographic verification,
   - validity and status checks, and
   - operational eligibility checks.

If issuer recognition fails, the credential is rejected without proceeding further.

##### Summary

For *Amadeus Board the Flight*, the Trusted Issuer List:

- acts as the **issuer‑recognition layer** of the trust framework;
- enables **cross‑border issuer recognition** without bilateral configuration; and
- supports realistic piloting while remaining aligned with the future eIDAS 2.0 / EUDI Wallet ecosystem.

It defines **who is trusted to issue what**, while the broader trust framework determines **whether a specific credential presentation should be accepted**.
