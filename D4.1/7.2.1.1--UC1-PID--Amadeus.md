#### PID in the Amadeus *Board the Flight* Use Case

In the **Board the Flight** use case, when a **Digital Travel Credential (DTC) is not mandatory**—for example, for **intra‑Schengen flights**—the **PID (Personal Identification Data)** stored in the traveller’s **EUDI Wallet** is used as the **alternative identity credential** to enable **online and remote identity verification** prior to airport arrival.

PID **does not have any obligation to contain biometric data**, which allows the following possibilties:

- **PID without biometric data**  
  Used for remote and manual identity verification (e.g. online check‑in), where the traveller shares non‑biometric attributes such as name, date of birth, or nationality.

- **PID with biometric data**  
  Used, with explicit traveller consent, to enable biometric processes (e.g. facial recognition for boarding), by binding a biometric reference to the traveller’s boarding entitlement.

In all cases, PID remains the **trusted identity anchor**, while the traveller retains full control over whether biometric data is included and shared.

**Example 1 – PID **without** biometric data**
Jose, a Spanish citizen living in **Madrid**, travels from **Madrid to Frankfurt**.  
During online check‑in, Jose remotely shares selected **PID attributes** from his wallet (e.g. full name, date of birth, nationality).

This enables:
- Remote identity verification
- Binding of booking and boarding credentials to Jose
- Selective disclosure with no biometric data involved

**Example 2 – PID **with** biometric data**
For the same trip, Jose’s PID also contains a **biometric reference** (e.g. facial image).  
With Jose’s explicit consent, the PID is used to:
- Bind a biometric token to his boarding entitlement
- Enable **biometric boarding** at the airport
- Avoid repeated document checks at the gate

In both cases, **PID is a precondition** of the use case and acts as the **trusted identity anchor**, while the traveller remains fully in control of what data is shared.
