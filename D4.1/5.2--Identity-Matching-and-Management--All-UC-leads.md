## Identity Matching and Management

### UC 1

Today, airline agent is obliged to visually check that the **name on the boarding pass matches the identity document**. Optionally verify that the **person presenting the document matches the portrait** on the identity card or passport

**Objective**
Ensure consistent and reliable identity matching across all use cases by linking a traveler’s digital identity to operational processes in a privacy‑preserving and legally compliant manner.

Manual visual checks are replaced by an **EUDIW‑based identity verification**.The traveler presents a **verifiable digital identity** (PID or DTC) from the wallet.Name matching and, where applicable, portrait/biometric matching are performed **digitally and consistently**, reducing reliance on manual checks while preserving compliance.

**Principles**

- User consent and data minimisation by design.
- Focus on **seamless user experience**, not on exposing technical complexity to end users.

### UC 2
Currently there is no shared or reusable identity layer across ETA-systems in various countries. This is challenging for backend systems of airports that need to communicate about ETAs with airlines and authorities from various countries. The ETA UC2 tries to harmonize this to relieve the burden of identity matching. Moreover, contrary to today’s ETA solutions that mostly take place in automated backend systems, by having an ETA in his or her EUDIW, it offers the traveler more management control and transparency.

### UC3

In **UC3: Seamless Digital Traveller Experience (SEDIT-X)**, identity matching is implemented as a **context-specific binding** between the traveller’s wallet-based identity and the operational object relevant to each step of the journey.

In the current baseline, this matching is still largely fragmented and sector-specific. Travellers repeatedly present identity documents, booking details, or travel entitlements to different service providers, and the link between identity and operational context is often established manually or through separate local systems.  

In the **airport episode**, identity matching goes beyond simple visual inspection of a name on a boarding pass. A  wallet-based flow is intended to support matching between:
- the traveller’s **PID / digital identity**,
- the **boarding pass credential** or airport-issued journey token,
- and, where applicable, a **biometric reference** used for **1:1 biometric verification** at selected checkpoints.  
This allows the airport to verify not only who the traveller is, but also whether that traveller is the person entitled to proceed for the relevant flight and process step. 

In the **hospitality episode**, identity matching is implemented by linking the verified traveller identity presented from the wallet to the corresponding **booking reference / reservation context**. The hotel-side process therefore does not rely only on manual inspection of identity documents, but on matching verified identity data and the **Booking Reference Credential** to the hotel reservation and check-in process.  

Across UC3, identity matching is expected to be performed either by the verifier itself or, more commonly, through the **EUDIW Intermediary Service**, which acts as the common layer connecting wallet-based identity presentation to airport, hotel, and other operational systems. In this sense, identity matching in SEDIT-X is not a standalone visual check, but part of a broader digitally mediated verification and entitlement-validation process.  

The overall objective in UC3 is therefore to move from repeated manual identity checks toward a more consistent and privacy-preserving model in which identity is matched digitally to the relevant journey context, while still allowing fallback or assisted processes where full system integration is not yet available.

### UC4

For UC 4 identity matching at airports is currently based on a badge system issued after employer driven onboarding and airport security checks. Identity is verified through document checks and background screening, and then linked to a role based access badge managed by the airport operator.

For visitors, identity is typically checked at registration by the hosting organisation using identity documents. In some cases a temporary badge is issued, but this is not always the case. Often access is instead handled through escorting, with no persistent identity record in the airport access system.

There is no shared or reusable identity layer across systems. Identity verification, badge issuance, and access control remain separate processes, and identity is not consistently reused across visits or contexts. Matching is therefore local and manual, based on each registration event rather than a standardised digital identity.


### UC5
For UC5 identity matching at the stadium is currently based on the clubs ticketing and account system. Fans create an N.E.C. account and buy a ticket through the existing club channels, and identity is linked to that account through basic registration and FastID wallet verification.

At the stadium, the ticket is used as the main access check. The link between the person and the ticket is mainly handled through the ticketing system, sometimes combined with biometric verification at the gate.

Employees, including interns and temporary staff, are managed through the club’s internal access setup. Their identity is linked to assigned roles and access rights defined by the organisation.

There is no fully shared identity layer between systems. Ticketing, identity verification, wallet usage and access control are still handled in separate parts of the setup. Matching is therefore based on club-specific accounts and ticket records rather than a fully reusable digital identity across different contexts.


### UC6

EUDIW improves Identity Matching by enabling secure binding of credentials to a verified digital identity. **Wallet Providers** ensure credentials are linked to the correct individual, while **Relying Parties** can trust that credentials received from certified wallets are valid and associated with that identity. They may also request core identity attributes to match against existing records when needed.
In some cases, full identity matching is unnecessary. For example, verifying a “student” status is sufficient to grant access to a discounted ticket, without identifying the individual. This selective disclosure reduces data sharing while maintaining trust.

### UC7-UC8

Currently, there is no formal mechanism for identity matching or verification between a passenger and their ticket. During the booking and purchase process, passengers are not required to authenticate their identity; instead, they must manually enter their personal data and provide physical certificates to prove eligibility for specific conditions, such as disability status for discounted fares or accompanying rights. Once on board, Trenitalia staff must manually verify each passenger's ticket against a physical ID. The introduction of the EUDIW would streamline both the booking phase and on-board identity verification, as passengers could share their credentials digitally, either via the app (remote presentation) or through proximity-based verification, ensuring a more efficient and secure process.

### UC9

In UC9, EUDI W identity matching and verification will be used to replace the Remote Proof of Identity and Liveness Verification (PVID) system used in the current solution when the traveller requests its biometric profile. Verified personal attributes shared from their EUDI Wallet will enable secure linkage with the traveller’s boarding information allowing passengers to navigate the airport journey using dedicated biometric-enabled checkpoints — from baggage drop-off, duty-free to boarding gates. The introduction of the EUDIW would streamline both the biometric profile credential issuance and its verifications in the airport.

### UC 11

In the current cross-border process, identity is established primarily through paper or scanned documents at each touch-point (MIFI, IRCC, institution, housing, transit operator), with no shared identifier linking the records. Identity matching across those touch-points is manual: each organisation keys the student into its own system from the documents presented, creating multiple unlinked records. Assurance level is implicit rather than explicit — a valid passport, CAQ, and study permit are treated as adequate proxies, with no formal mapping between eIDAS LoA and PCTF levels. Attribute updates (address, programme, status) propagate poorly across organisations, and the withdrawal or revocation of an identity artefact (for example a cancelled CAQ) is not digitally communicated downstream.

The EUDIW-based flow in UC 11 replaces several of these touch-points with a wallet presentation of PID and student EAA, reducing the number of unlinked records, making the assurance level explicit at each presentation, and supporting data minimisation through selective disclosure.
