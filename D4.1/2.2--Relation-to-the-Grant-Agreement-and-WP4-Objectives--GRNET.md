## Relation to the Grant Agreement and WP4 Objectives

### Introduction to the project

The APTITUDE project (Advanced Project for Trusted Identity Technologies and Unified Digital Ecosystem) was set up under the DIGITAL-2024-BESTUSE-TECH-06 call and formally established by Grant Agreement reference Ares(2025)4813623. The project brings together more than partners from 11 EU Member States and Ukraine, with the mission of analysing, integrating, and piloting use cases focused on Travel and Payment within the European Digital Identity Framework. APTITUDE is a Member-State-driven initiative that builds on foundational EU instruments, including the amendments to Regulation 910/2014 (the eIDAS 2.0 framework), the European Digital Identity Network, and the experience accumulated through earlier Large Scale Pilots. Its overall aim is to support the interoperable and scalable deployment of the European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW), helping Member States to meet the 2026 EUDIW deployment target set by the European Commission.

Within this broader project, WP4 is the Work Package whose main task is to pilot tickets and check-in scenarios. D4.1 fits into the sequence of three WP4 deliverables that will document the progression from specification to pilot execution over the 24-month project lifetime.

### WP4 objectives as stated in the Grant Agreement

The Grant Agreement defines WP4 as "Tickets and check-in", led by GRNET S.A., with a planned effort of 387.33 person-months across the 24 months of the project. Its main objective, as stated in the Grant Agreement, is:

> To successfully pilot the tickets and check-in use cases in compliance with the technical standards and specifications provided by WP2, the EU toolbox, and the reference implementation of the European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDIW).

From this high-level objective, several more specific commitments can be extracted. WP4 is expected to:

* Demonstrate that the EUDIW can be used for everyday travel and mobility activities, including the storage of boarding passes, the secure holding of travel tickets for trains and ferries, and the presentation of hotel booking confirmations upon arrival at a hotel.
* Address not only travel-focused scenarios, but also student access, recognising that student mobility across European campuses is both a significant real-world phenomenon (with more than 1.2 million ERASMUS students in 2022) and a welcoming ground for the application of wallet-based credentials.
* Work across three complementary scenarios, described in the Grant Agreement as **hotel check-ins**, **streamlined travel experience**, and **student access**. These scenarios are described in more detail later in this deliverable (Chapter 8), but the Grant Agreement already anchors the three-scenario structure that WP4 is bound to deliver against.
* Operate in a cross-border manner, performing interoperability tests between partner Member States to prove and improve the real-world interoperability of the EUDIW in the piloted scenarios.
* Comply with the technical outputs of WP2 (implementation profiles, testbed, trust infrastructure), with the EU toolbox, and with the reference implementation of the EUDIW. WP4 is explicitly positioned as a *consumer* of the standards and infrastructure produced elsewhere in the project.

### The three WP4 scenarios from the Grant Agreement

The Grant Agreement articulates the three scenarios that WP4 must pilot, summarised here as follows:

* **Scenario 1: Hotel check-ins.** A scenario that leverages the EUDIW to streamline hotel booking and guest check-in across Europe, addressing the pain points of manual data entry, physical document handling, long waiting times, inconsistent cross-border guest experiences, and natural language barriers at reception.
* **Scenario 2: Streamlined travel experience.** A scenario that uses the EUDIW to transform the passenger journey from booking to boarding across air, train, bus, and sea transport. The focus is on cross-border transportation, efficiency, user convenience, reduction of bureaucratic hurdles, minimisation of paper usage, and support for travellers with special needs. This scenario explicitly includes interaction with WP3 on passenger passport-certified information (DTCs) and with WP6 for payment steps where appropriate.
* **Scenario 3: Student access.** A scenario focused on the next-generation student card within the European Digital Identity portfolio. It covers the issuance of the International Student ID card (GYSC) to the EUDIW, cross-campus travel with student fares by bus (iDAKTO) and train (SNCF), receipt of the ERUA alliance credential as an EAA for ERASMUS students hosted by participating universities, and access to a wide range of campus services and discounts. The scenario builds on networks and initiatives such as ERASMUS, European Universities, EURAXESS, and aligns with the work of the European Student Card Initiative.

Cross-border interoperability is declared in the Grant Agreement as a transversal concern for all three scenarios, and WP4 will run a number of interoperability tests between partner Member States to demonstrate it in practice.

### The WP4 task structure and the position of D4.1

The Grant Agreement organises WP4 into four tasks, each producing specific outputs that together form the three WP4 deliverables:

* **T4.1 Coordination** (led by GRNET) ensures overall cooperation and information flow among WP4 participants, handles planning of coordination meetings, and manages interactions with the other Work Packages (notably WP2 and WP3) for alignment of activities.
* **T4.2 Stock taking and specifications** (led by GRNET, with contributions from all UC leads and the wider WP4 partner set) produces the content of **D4.1**, namely: user research and stakeholder interviews; the analysis of existing processes, technical implementations, and identity matching practices; the analysis of the legal framework; the detailed use case specifications; the technical specifications of interfaces towards the EUDIW and relying party systems; the definition of standards and protocols to be used in issuer and verifier flows; and the definition of user journeys.
* **T4.3 Implementation support** (under the same leadership with T4.2) prepares the piloting phase, provides technical and functional user support during piloting, and feeds into **D4.2** (UC full pilots and technical and implementation manual).
* **T4.4 Piloting, user testing and UC evaluation** runs the tests, documents the results, records the KPIs, and produces the final report, which constitutes **D4.3** (UC validation report).

D4.1 is therefore the direct output of T4.2 and the first of three sequential deliverables. The progression is deliberate:

1. **D4.1 (T4.2)** specifies the use cases, establishes the baseline, and documents the legal and interoperability requirements. It answers the question *what will be piloted and why*.
2. **D4.2 (T4.3)** will translate those specifications into a working implementation manual that partners can follow to build and integrate the pilot components. It answers the question *how the pilots will be built*.
3. **D4.3 (T4.4)** will report on the actual pilot execution, on user feedback, and on the KPI-based evaluation of whether the objectives declared in the Grant Agreement have been met. It will also document on learnings from the pilot phase and future directions. It basically answers the question *whether the pilots delivered on their promise*.

### Compliance anchors

There are three compliance references throughout this deliverable that constrain WP4 design choices:

* **WP2 technical standards and specifications.** WP4 implementations must align with the technical profiles and interoperability mechanisms defined by WP2. This includes conformance testing on the WP2 testbed prior to pilot execution.
* **The EU toolbox** for the European Digital Identity framework, as developed by the Member States and the European Commission, which provides the common reference architecture, reference standards, and common guidelines for wallet implementations.
* **The EUDIW reference implementation**, which serves as the baseline implementation against which WP4 partners validate their integrations, ensuring that the piloted use cases can realistically be replicated on production wallet instances issued by Member States.

By anchoring WP4 to these three references, the Grant Agreement ensures that the WP4 pilots are not isolated experiments but contributions to a converging European Digital Identity ecosystem. D4.1 records, in its later chapters, how each of the 11 accepted use cases maps to these compliance anchors, and identifies the areas where further coordination with WP2, with the reference implementation maintainers, or with the relevant standardisation bodies is needed before implementation can begin.
